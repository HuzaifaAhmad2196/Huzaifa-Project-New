Developmental Progressions - What You Need to Know about Development in Your Child

Understanding Your Child's Unique Language Journey

Language development is a course, but the path of a particular child is his/her own. Cooing and babbling are the starting stages where the baby can use gestures and sounds to interact with the caregivers. At about 1 year they can frequently come up with the first recognisable words, normally names of people or common items. During the toddler age, vocabulary grows at a very fast rate and the two word phrases develop. At the age of three, children are expected to speak in sentences and also enjoy telling stories. Preschool age kids start playing with humour, rhymes and more sophisticated grammar and at the first school years, most children are able to narrate elaborate stories and engage in long conversations.

Important: These are not strict schedules; instead, they are guidelines to the types of skills that children normally acquire. The EYLF helps us to recall the fact that children learn differently and at various rates and that learning is a process that should be praised as progressive. Milestones are just useful pointers that families need to take into account but should not be in the form of a set of strict checklists, with families observing possible difficulties.

👶 Birth to 12 Months

Building the Foundation

What Children Are Learning:

  • Recognizing familiar voices and responding to their name
  • Making different sounds and babbling (like "bababa" or "dadada")
  • Understanding that communication is back-and-forth - taking turns in "conversations"
  • Using gestures like waving, pointing, or reaching
  • Understanding simple words and phrases like "no" or "bye-bye"
  • Responding to music, songs, and tone of voice
💡 How You Can Help: Talk to your baby throughout the day, narrating what you're doing. Respond to their sounds and babbling as if you're having a conversation. Sing songs, read books, and make eye contact.

🧒 12 to 24 Months

First Words and Beyond

What Children Are Learning:

  • Saying their first words (around 12 months)
  • Understanding much more than they can say
  • Following simple instructions like "get your shoes"
  • Combining words into simple two-word phrases by age 2 (like "more juice" or "daddy gone")
  • Rapidly learning new words - vocabulary can grow from a few words to 50+ words or more
  • Using language for different purposes - requesting, greeting, commenting
  • Enjoying stories and simple books
💡 How You Can Help: Label objects and actions during daily routines. Expand on what your child says - if they say "dog," you might say "Yes, that's a big brown dog!" Read together every day and let them point to pictures.

👧 2 to 3 Years

Sentences and Stories

What Children Are Learning:

  • Using 3-4 word sentences and asking lots of questions
  • Vocabulary expanding to several hundred words
  • Using different sentence types - statements, questions, negatives
  • Talking about things that aren't present - past events, future plans
  • Following two-step instructions
  • Starting to have back-and-forth conversations
  • Using language in pretend play
  • Most speech is understandable to familiar people (though some sounds may still be developing)
💡 How You Can Help: Ask open-ended questions like "What happened?" instead of yes/no questions. Talk about past and future events. Engage in pretend play together. Be patient when they're trying to tell you something.

🧑 3 to 5 Years

Complex Language and Literacy Readiness

What Children Are Learning:

  • Using complex sentences with connecting words like "because," "when," "but"
  • Telling simple stories with a beginning, middle, and end
  • Understanding and using more abstract vocabulary - emotions, time concepts, categories
  • Following multi-step instructions
  • Speech is clear and understood by most people, even strangers
  • Engaging in longer conversations, staying on topic
  • Understanding humor, sarcasm (beginning), and figurative language (starting to develop)
  • Showing interest in letters, sounds, and print - building literacy skills
  • Adjusting language for different listeners (talking differently to a baby vs. an adult)
💡 How You Can Help: Have longer conversations about topics of interest. Read chapter books together. Encourage storytelling. Play word games and rhyming games. Answer their "why" questions thoughtfully.

👦 5+ Years

Sophisticated Communication

What Children Are Learning:

  • Using grammar correctly most of the time
  • Understanding and telling detailed stories
  • Using language to reason, problem-solve, and think critically
  • Understanding multiple meanings of words and jokes
  • Participating effectively in group conversations
  • Using and understanding more sophisticated vocabulary
  • Connecting spoken language to reading and writing
  • Understanding social rules of communication in different settings
💡 How You Can Help: Continue having rich conversations. Discuss books, movies, and experiences. Encourage them to explain their thinking. Support their reading and writing development. Model sophisticated language.

Developing Reading and Writing

The concept of reading and writing does not initiate immediately children enter school. Rather they are created over time through a playful and meaningful interaction with print and symbols. This can be referred to as emergent literacy.

📝 Early Mark Making

The Foundation of Writing

What Children Are Learning:

  • The first phase could be the scribble of a toddler and this indicates that he or she is trying to make marks
  • These marks are later on more deliberate looking like letters and shapes
  • Children also begin to identify print in the environment, e.g. signs, labels, their name
  • At this point, they start to associate sounds and letters, play with fake spelling, and start trying to write sentences or words
  • These early stages are supported and encouraged and result in confident reading and writing
💡 How You Can Help: This process can be guided by families that are able to provide children with books, draw and write materials, as well as to associate verbal and written forms. Reading out loud every day, indicating words in stories and asking children to write down shopping lists or birthday cards, although they may use an invented spelling, makes them understand the relationship between spoken language and written language.

📚 Framework Connections

EYLF and Australian Curriculum Links

Early Years Learning Framework:

  • Early attempts at mark-making and storytelling as a form of real communication are valued by the EYLF (Outcome 5)
  • This is the same focus of the Australian Curriculum (English Foundation Year 2) which puts emphasis on phonics, oral language and early reading and writing in real-life situations

🌈 Special Considerations

Multilingual Development

Children learning more than one language may have slightly different patterns of development, and this is completely normal and healthy. They might:

  • Mix languages in the same sentence (called code-switching) - this is a sign of sophisticated language skills
  • Have a larger overall vocabulary across both languages than in just one language
  • Go through periods of preferring one language
  • Take a bit longer to start talking, but catch up quickly

Remember: Learning multiple languages is a gift! Keep using your home language - it supports all language development.

Individual Differences

Every child is unique! Some children are naturally more talkative, while others are more reserved. Some learn language in bursts, others steadily. What matters is:

  • Your child is making progress over time
  • Your child is communicating effectively for their age (even if not perfectly)
  • Your child is engaged and interested in communication
  • You feel confident in your child's ability to express needs and connect with others

When to Seek Support

Trust your instincts as a parent. Consider talking to a professional if:

  • Your child isn't babbling or making sounds by 12 months
  • Your child isn't using words by 18 months
  • Your child isn't combining words by 2-2.5 years
  • Your child loses language skills they previously had
  • Your child's speech is very difficult to understand after age 3
  • Your child seems frustrated trying to communicate
  • You have concerns about your child's hearing

Early intervention works! If you have concerns, reach out to your doctor or a speech pathologist. There's no harm in asking questions.
